Phishing URLs and internet domains

Hackers use disguised links to lure their victims to fake or infected websites. In this chapter you will learn how to decipher the links of any attack.

What Could A Phishing URL Look Like?

The attacker must intelligently choose the domain names as the goal is to convince users that the web address is authentic. Let’s analyze the phishing example below.

http://   paypal . com-webappsuserid29348325limited . active-userid.com   /webapps/89980/
protocol http://
Domain name active-userid.com
path /webapps/89980/
Subdomain item1 com-webappsuserid29348325limited
Subdomain item2 paypal

Although the real domain name is active-userid.com, the attacker tried to make the domain look like paypal.com by adding FreeURL. When users see paypal.com at the beginning of the URL, they may easily decide to trust the site and even share their sensitive information with it. This method is frequently used by attackers.

Cybersquatting And Typosquatting

Other methods that are often used by attackers are Cybersquatting and Typosquatting.

  • Cybersquatting (also known as domain squatting) involves registering, marketing, or using a domain name with ill intent to profit from the goodwill of a trademark belonging to someone else. Let’s say the name of your company is “abcompany,” and you register your domain as abcompany.com. If you neglect to purchase abcompany.net, abcompany.org, and abcompany.biz, phishers can easily register them as their own and use them for fraudulent purposes such as phishing. The cybersquatter may also offer to sell these additional domains to you at an inflated price.
  • Typosquatting, also called URL hijacking, is a form of cybersquatting which exploits typographical errors made by Internet users when inputting a website address into a web browser. Most of these errors are hard to notice while quick reading, hence the danger. URLs created by Typosquatters are almost indistinguishable from those of their respective trusted domains. A user may accidentally enter an incorrect website address or click a link which looks like a trusted domain and end up visiting an alternative website owned by a phisher.

A famous example of Typosquatting is goggle.com, an extremely dangerous website. Another similar one is yutube.com, which targets Youtube users.

Similarly, www.airfrance.com has been typosquatted as www.arifrance.com, diverting users to a website peddling discount travel.

Other examples include paywpal.com, microroft.com, applle.com, appie.com.

Subdomains

If you visit a website and notice “www1”, “www2”, or other “www#” in the URL, what does this mean? Simply put, very popular websites may have multiple servers working in a load-balancing configuration to serve content when requested. Therefore, the respective company may use this numbering strategy for clarity. Seeing a numbered server in a URL is not in itself an indicator of a phishing site.

To help users quickly determine the top-level and secondary domains within a URL, many companies and organizations have started to use “domain highlighting.” When a user visits a site, part of the URL will dim after a few seconds, leaving the top-level and secondary domains dark. For example:

PayPal, Inc. (US) | https://www.paypal.com/home

It’s a good habit to always look for the signs that mark a website legitimate and secure:

  • the closed padlock,
  • https://, and
  • the company name highlighted within the URL. Some browsers may also add the company name in green in front of the URL (as in the PayPal example above).

If a site’s certificate is expired or otherwise invalid, some browsers, such as Internet Explorer and Firefox, or security services will warn users. If you wonder whether it is safe to proceed through the warning, use other available indicators (review the URL again) to help determine whether the site is legitimate. If in doubt, do not proceed.

More URL Examples

Now that you’re armed with this background information, let’s look at some more examples.

  • Nowadays, people are fairly used to seeing and using commercial sites, such as: http://www.amazon.com. This is a well-known site, and the URL doesn’t include any suspicious modifications.
  • URLs can be formed in almost any fashion, allowing website owners to build unique site names. Unfortunately, this also makes it easy for phishers to do the same, meaning they can build site names that closely resemble legitimate and trusted websites. For example, a simple period can make a lot of difference:
    http://www.ama.zon.com/gp/cart/view.html/ref=nav_cart

    If a person were to click on the link above, instead of going to amazon.com they will be directed to zon.com, which could be a site registered by phishers.

  • Don’t trust links with the “@” symbol in the URL, like in this example: http://amazon.com@66.161.153.155/catalog

    Clicking on the link above will direct you to the IP address 66.161.153.155, not to amazon.com. And if you see the “@” symbol, be particularly careful as phishers routinely use this URL-manipulation tactic.

  • What about this type of URL? http://209.131.36.158/amazon.com/index.jsp

    Similarly to what we saw in #3 above, this URL will direct the user to the specified IP address, not amazon.com which is listed after the first single forward slash.

  • What if you see a URL similar to the one below – whether you’re asked to click it or you notice it loading in your browser: http://www.google.com/url?q=http://www.badsite.com

    This sample URL will refer a person from one site (in this case, google.com) to another, i.e., badsite.com (note the “=http://” nomenclature which allows this). Referrals are not in themselves bad, but a referral could lead to a phishing site. In this case, badsite.com doesn’t look to be legitimate.
